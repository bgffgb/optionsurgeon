Option Pricing

Having a bit of understanding of option pricing helps clear up why buying/selling options can be profitable. The short version is, option prices are set by market makers. These are institutions providing liquidity by two-sided markets (willing to both sell and buy) during exchange hours. In return, they have some special privileges: reduced trading fees, ability to naked short, etc. Market makers don't speculate on the future price of the underlying asset - rather, they maintain a risk neutral position, making a small percentage on every option contract filled, whether the price goes up, down or flatlines.

This option pricing is a mechanical system, following some option pricing model like Black-Scholes or Levy distribution, done in real-time. Most of these models assume A) the asset price will stay stable in the future, be it over the next week or year B) predict the potential magnitude of price movements based on historical price volatilty.

I highly recommend Warren Buffet's take on Black Scholes, it does a great job pointing out the weaknesses in mechanical option pricing. It is a "know-nothing affair" - so as long as you have better understanding of the fundamentals, future growth or potential catastrophic downfall of an underlying asset, you can find mispriced options. Unlike market makers, you have the option to pick and choose which positions to enter; market makers might be pricing options correctly 99% of the time, but you get to choose your trades, waiting for that 1% of opportunity.

Option-Implied Risk-Neutral Distribution (RND)

Option prices are already pricing in potential future price movements. If you take all the Call and Put option prices across all strikes for a given expiration date and turn it into a PhD worthy math problem, you can get back the priced in probability distribution of the underlying reaching certain strikes by expiry. It's called the Option-Implied Risk-Neutral Distribution (RND) and is pretty good baseline most of the time.

RND estimation in practice

So how do you get this distribution? If you went through the math, you'll know it's a pretty heavy non-linear optimization problem with no closed-form solution. You unfortunately can't solve it in real-time from an option chain, and most people (like me) are not willing to wait several minutes for the computations to run. Limitations

  • The 'fair' price of an option is the middle point between the Bid and Ask prices; in reality, the 'fair' price is probably closer to the Bid price, favoring option sellers. This bias is somewhat reduced by using Call/Put spreads to estimate probabilities, but it still is a source of imprecision, especially with wide Bid/Ask spreads
  • Later dated option expiries carry more uncertainty and have generally higher Bid/Ask spreads; RND estimates tend to be less accurate in those cases
  • Sometimes options are just priced weirdly; Bid/Ask spreads can be crazy wide and pricing terribly inconsistent; I present to you one such curiosity, GME January 2023 expiry distribution:

For all these reasons mentioned above, the fitted RND might seemingly promise great returns on some options. Obviously, you cannot trust it. It can be due to fitting errors, or options are just priced particularly unfavorably. Case in point, here is ae the expected returns from GME class for January 2023 expiry:

If you look at the cumulative distribution funciton from the previous figure, it should be clear why. The data is extremely noisy, there isn't a single, unique distribution that gives a perfect fit over that set of data points. If the fitted RND models would be perfect, all expectations across all puts and calls would be 0.

You cannot base your investment decisions on the expectation estimates given by the fitted RND estimate; the RND is just a simple indicator, a starting point to visualize (roughly) the priced in probabilities, not a magical shortcut to easy trades.
